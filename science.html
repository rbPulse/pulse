The Science

Peptides are not new.
Understanding them is.

A straightforward guide to what peptides are, how they work, and why the delivery method changes everything.

01 / What is a peptide

Your body runs on peptides.

Peptides are short chains of amino acids — the same building blocks as proteins, just smaller. Your body produces thousands of them naturally. They act as signaling molecules: chemical messages that tell your cells what to do and when to do it.

Growth hormone release, tissue repair, sleep regulation, immune response — all of it is coordinated, in part, by peptides. They're not foreign to your biology. They are your biology.

Supplemental peptides mimic or amplify these signals. They don't override your system. They speak its language.

Unlike anabolic steroids, which substitute for hormones or force receptor saturation, peptides work upstream — nudging your own systems to produce more of what they already make.

02 / How they work

Receptor binding. Precise by design.

Each peptide is structured to bind to a specific receptor. That specificity is what makes peptides different from broad-spectrum compounds. A peptide targeting growth hormone secretagogue receptors won't interact with your cortisol system. A repair-focused peptide like BPC-157 works locally at the site of tissue damage — it doesn't flood your entire endocrine system.

This precision is why side effect profiles for therapeutic peptides are generally favorable compared to traditional performance compounds. You're not hitting everything at once. You're sending a targeted signal to a specific system.

BPC-157, for example, has been shown in peer-reviewed research to accelerate healing of tendons, ligaments, muscle, and gut tissue — with no observed toxicity at therapeutic doses. It occurs naturally in gastric juice.

04 / Protocol compliance

The research works. Most people don't finish.

The scientific literature on therapeutic peptides is genuinely compelling. The gap between what the research shows and what most users experience isn't about efficacy. It's about compliance.

Peptides require consistent subcutaneous administration — typically daily or multiple times per week — across cycles of 4 to 12 weeks. In practice, this means reconstituting lyophilized powder with bacteriostatic water, calculating doses by weight, drawing into an insulin syringe, and repeating this process without error, indefinitely.

The friction is real. Most people stop not because the protocol isn't working, but because the workflow breaks down. Missed doses compound. Inconsistent dosing produces inconsistent results. The conclusion drawn is that peptides don't work — when the actual problem was delivery.

Pulse was built to close this gap. Pre-filled pods eliminate reconstitution. The auto-injector pen removes the syringe. The 30/60/90-day structure removes the guesswork. The compound still does what the research says it does — because now it actually gets administered correctly.

05 / Safety and oversight

Where the science stands.

Most therapeutic peptides used in performance and longevity protocols exist in a research-use context. Several — like Tesamorelin — are fully FDA-approved for specific indications. Others have extensive peer-reviewed preclinical data and growing clinical use, but have not completed the full FDA approval pathway.

This doesn't mean they're experimental in the colloquial sense. BPC-157 has been in research since the 1990s. Epithalon has decades of study behind it. The compounds Pulse uses are not novel or untested — they are well-characterized molecules with defined mechanisms and documented safety profiles at therapeutic doses.

Every Pulse protocol is reviewed by a licensed physician before it ships. Dosing is set within evidence-based ranges. This is not a workaround — it's the standard of care that should have existed from the beginning.
